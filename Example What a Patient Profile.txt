1️⃣ Example: What a Patient Profile Looks Like (Real-life)

Think of a patient profile as a multi-section report for ONE subject.

🧑‍⚕️ Patient Profile – Example Structure

Header

Study ID

Subject ID

Treatment Arm

Randomization Date

📌 Section 1: Demographics

Age

Sex

Race

Country / Site

👉 Who is the patient?

📌 Section 2: Medical History

Chronic conditions

Start/End dates

Ongoing conditions

👉 What conditions did the patient have before the study?

📌 Section 3: Treatment & Dosing

Study drug start/stop dates

Dose changes

Interruptions

👉 What treatment did they receive?

📌 Section 4: Adverse Events

AE term

Start/End date

Severity

Serious or not

Relationship to drug

👉 Any safety issues?

📌 Section 5: Concomitant Medications

Medication name

Start/End date

Indication

👉 Any other medicines taken?

📌 Section 6: Labs / Vitals / ECG

Normal vs abnormal values

Flags for out-of-range results

👉 Any clinical abnormalities?

📌 Section 7: Efficacy (e.g., Pain – NPRS)

Baseline score

Visit-wise scores

Trend over time

👉 Did the patient improve or worsen?

📌 All sections are shown in date/time order, so the reviewer sees the full story clearly.

3️⃣ Difference: Patient Profile vs Subject Listing (VERY important)

Interviewers LOVE this question.

🆚 Patient Profile vs Subject Listing

| Patient Profile          | Subject Listing        |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------- |
| One subject              | Many subjects          |
| Complete story           | Tabular data           |
| Multiple sections        | Usually one domain     |
| Used by medical reviewer | Used by DM / reviewers |
| Chronological            | Row-based              |

🍋 Lemon version

Patient profile = full movie of one patient 🎬

Subject listing = Excel sheet of many patients 📊

4️⃣ One-line interview answers (memorize)
🔹 Patient Profile

“A patient profile is a subject-level, chronological summary combining data across domains to support medical review.”

🔹 Subject Listing

“A subject listing is a tabular display of clinical data across multiple subjects used for data review and quality checks.”

5️⃣ Bonus line (sounds senior 👌)

“Patient profiles are designed to help reviewers quickly identify safety concerns, trends, and inconsistencies at the subject level.”

“In patient profiles, I create a subject-level summary by combining data from multiple domains into a chronological, reviewer-friendly format. I ensure data consistency, correct sequencing of events, and highlight important safety and protocol-related information to support medical review.”

“I pull data from domains like demographics, medical history, adverse events, concomitant medications, labs, vitals, and efficacy assessments. I align dates and times, sort events chronologically, and format the output so reviewers can easily understand the subject’s study journey.”
